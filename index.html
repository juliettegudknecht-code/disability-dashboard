For nearly five decades, the number of children and students served under IDEA has continued to rise.

In School Year 1976–77, the first year of reporting, about 3.7 million children ages 3 through 21 were served under IDEA, Part B. By 2024–25 the count was about 8.2 million. Measured against public-school enrollment, the share served rose from roughly 8 percent to about 15 percent.

Specific learning disability is by far the most prevalent category.

In SY 2024–25, the most prevalent disability category of school-age students served under IDEA, Part B, was specific learning disability (33.5 percent). The next most common were speech or language impairment (17.4 percent), other health impairment (16.4 percent), and autism (14.8 percent). Other health impairment and autism have grown the fastest among the categories.

Since School Year 2000–01, several categories have grown while others held steady or declined. Other health impairment and autism each grew to about 1.2 million by 2024–25, and developmental delay roughly tripled, while intellectual disability fell and specific learning disability edged down.

More students than ever spend most of the school day in a regular classroom.

From SY 2019–20 to SY 2023–24, the share of school-age students served under IDEA, Part B, educated inside the regular class 80 percent or more of the day rose from 64.8 percent to 67.8 percent, while the most restrictive settings kept shrinking. Over the longer run, the Department reports this share climbed from 33.1 percent in 1990 to 67.8 percent in 2023.

How much of the day is spent in a regular classroom depends on the disability.

In SY 2023–24, more than 8 in 10 students (88.7 percent) reported under speech or language impairment were educated inside the regular class 80 percent or more of the day. For intellectual disability it was just over 2 in 10 (20.3 percent), and for multiple disabilities, less than 2 in 10 (16.2 percent).

About 1 in 7.5 children are served under IDEA, and the share dips in the teen years.

Set against the U.S. population, the children served under IDEA, Part B, are a sizable share of all children. Of the roughly 54.6 million children ages 5 through 17 in the 2024 American Community Survey, about 7.2 million were served under IDEA, Part B. Students served are also more likely to be male: 64.7 percent, against 49.5 percent of the U.S. population.

Nearly 9 in 10 infants and toddlers receive early intervention in the home.

In SY 2024–25, of the 458,920 infants and toddlers served under IDEA, Part C, 88.7 percent received their early intervention services primarily in the home. A community-based setting was the primary setting for 7.8 percent.

More students who leave are graduating with a diploma, and fewer are dropping out.

In School Year 2022–23, 75.8 percent of students ages 14 through 21 who exited services under IDEA, Part B, and school graduated with a regular high school diploma, up from 66.1 percent in 2014–15. Over the same period, the share who dropped out fell from 18.4 percent to 13.9 percent. Measured over decades, the diploma share is up from about 42 percent in 1987–88.

About 2/3 of states need assistance or intervention under Part B.

Each year the Department issues a determination on how well each state carries out IDEA, based on its results and compliance. In the latest (2026) determinations, 21 of 60 states and reporting entities met the requirements under Part B, while 6 needed intervention; a year earlier, in 2025, 20 met and only 2 needed intervention. This page has real IDEA-related data and counts, reported by states and OSEP every year.

Under Section 618, states report annual counts on the children and students with disabilities they serve: child count and educational environments, discipline, exiting, personnel, assessment, and the Part C early intervention collections. A few figures draw on related federal sources, including the U.S. Census Bureau’s American Community Survey for population context, the Department’s 2025 determination letters, and the IDEA Part B Maintenance of Effort and CEIS collection for funding.
