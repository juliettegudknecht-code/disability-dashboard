The IDEA Section 618 Data Collections Story: fifty years of counting every child served

Section 618 of IDEA requires each state to report data every year on the infants, toddlers, children, and students with disabilities it serves. The figures below draw on those public reports. All figures are reported counts, not estimates.

The number of children and students served under IDEA has risen for nearly five decades.

In School Year 1976–77, the first year of reporting, about 3.7 million children ages 3 through 21 were served under IDEA, Part B. By 2024–25 the count was about 8.2 million. Measured against public-school enrollment, the share served rose from roughly 8 percent to about 15 percent.

Specific learning disability is the most prevalent category. Autism is now the fourth.

In SY 2023–24, the most prevalent disability category of school-age students served under IDEA, Part B, was specific learning disability (33.8 percent). The next most common were speech or language impairment (17.9 percent), other health impairment (16.5 percent), and autism (13.8 percent).

Autism has grown faster than any other category.

When autism was first reported as its own category in School Year 2000–01, about 93,000 students were served under it. By 2024–25 the count exceeded 1.2 million. Over the same period, most other categories held steady or declined.

A growing majority spend most of the school day in a regular classroom.

From SY 2019–20 to SY 2023–24, the share of school-age students served under IDEA, Part B, educated inside the regular class 80 percent or more of the day rose from 64.8 percent to 67.8 percent, while the most restrictive settings kept shrinking.

How much of the day is spent in a regular classroom depends on the disability.

In SY 2023–24, more than 8 in 10 students (88.7 percent) reported under speech or language impairment were educated inside the regular class 80 percent or more of the day. For intellectual disability it was just over 2 in 10 (20.3 percent), and for multiple disabilities, less than 2 in 10 (16.2 percent).

Nearly 9 in 10 infants and toddlers receive early intervention in the home.

In SY 2023–24, of the 462,847 infants and toddlers served under IDEA, Part C, 89.3 percent received their early intervention services primarily in the home. A community-based setting was the primary setting for 7.2 percent.

Graduation has risen and dropping out has fallen among students who exit services.

In School Year 2022–23, 75.8 percent of students ages 14 through 21 who exited services under IDEA, Part B, and school graduated with a regular high school diploma, up from 66.1 percent in 2014–15. Over the same period, the share who dropped out fell from 18.4 percent to 13.9 percent.

About two-thirds of states need assistance or intervention under Part B.

Each year the Department issues a determination on how well each state carries out IDEA, based on its results and compliance. For Part B in 2026, 22 of 64 states and reporting entities met requirements; 36 needed assistance and 6 needed intervention. The Part C determinations, for early intervention, were somewhat stronger.

About 1 in 7.5 children are served under IDEA, and the share dips in the teen years.

Set against the U.S. population, the children served under IDEA, Part B, are a sizable share of all children. Of the roughly 54.6 million children ages 5 through 17 in the 2024 American Community Survey, about 7.2 million, or 1 in 7.5, were served under IDEA, Part B. Students served are also more likely to be male: 64.7 percent, against 49.5 percent of the U.S. population.

Follow the money: where a state’s reported IDEA, Part B allocation goes.

In the Maintenance of Effort and Coordinated Early Intervening Services (CEIS) collection, each district reports its Section 611 (school-age) and Section 619 (preschool) allocation. A share may be set aside for CEIS; the rest pays for special education and related services. Pick a state or one of its largest districts to see the reported allocation flow.

Most figures here are counts reported by states under IDEA Section 618.

Under Section 618, states report annual counts on the children and students with disabilities they serve: child count and educational environments, discipline, exiting, personnel, assessment, and the Part C early intervention collections. A few figures draw on related federal sources, including the U.S. Census Bureau’s American Community Survey for population context, the Department’s 2026 determination letters, and the IDEA Part B Maintenance of Effort and CEIS collection for funding.
